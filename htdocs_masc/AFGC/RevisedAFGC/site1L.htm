A Functional Analysis of the Arabidopsis Genome via Global Gene Expression Analysis and Gene Disruption

(January 1, 1999 toDecember 31, 2002)

         In the near future, the complete sequence of the Arabidopsis genome will be known and this together with cDNA sequencing will lead to the identification of nearly all Arabidopsis genes. Most of these genes will have homologs in other plants and many will have unknown function. The overall goal of this project is to provide plant biologists with a set of powerful tools that can be used in their efforts to understand the function and interrelationships of the 20,000 or more genes in this and other plants.

        Our project has three components: technology development, service to the community, and biological application. It is being executed by the Arabidopsis Functional Genomics Consortium (AFGC) which is comprised of a team of investigators from four institutions: Michigan State University (Pam Green, PI, and Ken Keegstra and John Ohlrogge, co-PIs), University of Wisconsin, Madison (Mike Sussman and Rick Amasino, co-PIs), Yale University (Steve Dellaporta, co-PI), Carnegie Institute of Washington at Stanford University (Shauna Somerville and Mike Cherry, co-PIs).

         Two synergistic technologies to investigate gene function are being developed and provided at service facilities. The microarray facility will assemble DNA microarrays of many of the genes of Arabidopsis. By hybridizing these arrays with probes corresponding to mRNA from different tissues and organs, or from plants subjected to different stimuli, global gene expression patterns can be investigated. We are also setting up database and software support to facilitate sophisticated searching and comparisons among these global expression patterns. The community will be provided access through the WWW to AFGC microarray data within the Stanford Genetics Microarray database. In parallel with our microarray experiments, our second technology development effort has been to optimize high throughput screening of Arabidopsis plants for T-DNA insertion mutations ("gene knockouts"). This has enabled the establishment of a gene knockout facility. We are also testing new ways to further maximize the efficiency of identifying gene knockouts that will rely on the use of junction fragments. Both the gene knockout facility and the microarray facility comprise the service component of our project. These technologies will be made available to the academic plant biology community beginning in year two of the project (during the year 2000) and with "no strings attached" with respect to intellectual property. The coordinator of the DNA Microarray facility is Ellen Wisman and the coordinator of the Knockout Facility is Sandra Austin-Phillips.

        The biological application component of this proposal will focus on enhancing our understanding of plant-specific genes of unknown function, but it will generate a large amount of data on the other Arabidopsis genes as well. Plant-specific genes, i.e. genes found in Arabidopsis and other plants but not in yeast, bacterial, or animal systems, are of great interest because they may be involved in plant-specific processes such as plant-pathogen interactions, cell-wall biosynthesis, and others. Using our bioinformatics expertise, we will identify and incorporate onto the AFGC microarrays, many of the plant-specific genes of Arabidopsis. We will then examine their expression in the major organs of Arabidopsis and under at least 20 different conditions. Knockout mutations will be identified in a number of these genes and the mutant plants will be examined for altered phenotypes. The microarray data for all Arabidopsis genes studied in this project will be available through the WWW from a database specifically designed for that purpose. Summaries of the plant-specific gene data will also be provided which will be hyperlinked, via the WWW, back to data within the microarray database. Seed from the mutants will be distributed through ABRC. This will allow members of the community to formulate and test hypotheses about the function of these plant-specific genes.

         Taken together, the information and materials generated from this project should help advance plant genome research to the next level. Just as the Arabidopsis EST project benefitted ongoing research in a variety of plant systems, the impact of this project is expected to extend far beyond Arabidopsis. This is particularly true for the information obtained about plant-specific genes, many of which will be of general significance to crop plants and other plants of commercial value.

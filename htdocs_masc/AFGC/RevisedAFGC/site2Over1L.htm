    Organization and Equipment

    We operate as a single facility with two sites: MSU and Carnegie. Dr. Ellen Wisman at MSU will coordinate experiments to be done at the two sites with Dr. Shauna Somerville, who leads the AFGC Microarray team at the Carnegie. Both sites have identical equipment. The gridding robots were purchased from Gene Machines and are similar to that developed in the Brown lab. Telechem tips are used by the robots to spot the DNA. Hybridization signals are detected with a Scan Array 3000 or 4000 or an Axon 4000B Scanner.


    • Origin of DNAs

    Most DNAs on AFGC arrays were prepared by PCR amplification of cDNA inserts from a nonredundant set of MSU EST clones. Please note that the DNA preparations used as PCR templates were from the same tubes used for the original EST sequencing performed by Tom Newman [Newman et al., 1994, Genes Galore: A summary of methods for accessing results from partial sequencing of anonymous Arabidopsis cDNA clones. Plant Physiol., 106:1241-1255]. This unique resource, kindly made available to the AFGC by Tom Newman, has allowed us to avoid handling errors often associated with large gene sets. A random selection of approximately 1,100 of the EST clones used for arrays were recently sequenced from AFGC stocks and 99% matched their original designation (see User's Guide: Sequence Verification. Please note that the AFGC microarray DNAs are not derived from the Arabidopsis Biological Resource Center (ABRC) stocks. However, all clones can be requested from ABRC. Users are encouraged to confirm the identity of requested clones upon receipt. A small fraction of DNA clones amplified for AFGC arrays were clones supplied by users. The Y2001 microarray arrays also contain 3,000 amplicons prepared by PCR amplification from genomic DNA with gene-specific primers.


    Number of DNAs on AFGC Arrays

    The Year 2000 array produced by the AFGC contains DNAs representing about 11,000 ESTs from the MSU collection representing approximately 7,500 genes. The Year 2001 array consists of about 14,000 unique elements.


    Replicates

    In the past, the AFGC has offered two microarrays for each experiment to be used as biological replicates and/or as technical replicates of the same two-sample comparison. In cycles 4 & 5, investigators had the option of requesting up to four microarray slides for each experiment (Note: 2 RNA samples are required for each microarray).

    Both biological variation in the plant materials and technical variation arising from the handling steps (printing variation, labeling variation, hybridization variation) will contribute noise to the intensity measurements associated with each spot or gene. AFGC has observed that using technical replicates with a "reverse labeling" strategy can substantially reduce false positive results. Biological replicates are also very important to define genes that vary due to biological variation.

    The number of replications will depend heavily on the type of experiment and the goals of the experimenter. For example, our experience has shown that up to 25% of the genes in the array differ in expression by more than 2-fold in comparisons of different tissues. In such comparisons, false positives represent a very minor proportion of the differentially expressed genes. However, in some comparisons of mutant to wild-type, less than one percent of the genes on the array show a differential expression of 2-fold. Although in both experiments, the number of false positives may be the same, the conclusions of the mutant/wild-type comparison may be much more severely impacted.

    An additional analysis of the impact of replications on microarray data is available in the paper: Lee ML, Kuo FC, Whitmore GA, Sklar J. (2000) Importance of replication in microarray gene expression studies: statistical methods and evidence from repetitive cDNA hybridizations. Proc Natl Acad Sci U S A. 97:9834-9.

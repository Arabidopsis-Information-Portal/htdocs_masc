Sector Bias

Updated August 1, 2002.

Sector bias is bias detected in the spatial distribution of ratios in an array [in The Stanford Microarray Database (SMD) see: Display Data/View Array Details/Ratios On Arrays]. The AFGC arrays are printed in either 32 or 48 sectors. Since the AFGC EST clones were not printed in any specific order, our assumption in this analysis is that the average of ratios in one sector should be roughly equivalent to the average of all ratios on the array (Note: flagged spots and control spots are excluded from this analysis). This assumption is tested using ANOVA (analysis of variance), and the statistic R2 (see note below) is used to provide a measure of the "magnitude" of the spatial bias. For example, an R2 value of 0.15 can be interpreted as saying that 15% of the variation in expression ratios can be attributed to sector. Significant deviation from a random spatial pattern of ratios is indicative of a technical problem (e.g., in the hybridization and washing or in the scanning of slides). Replicated experiments with significant sector bias show poor correlation values in the plot "Compare log2 (RATN) with experiment from the same print". Note, however, that replicated experiments with similar patterns of spatial bias will exhibit high and misleading correlation values in the "Compare log2 (RATN) . . . " plot.

We have examined AFGC slides for sector bias. Slides with significant sector bias have been retained in the SMD as it may be possible to recover useful data from some sectors of such slides. However, the slide names (labeled "Experiment" in SMD) of slides with sector R2 values of greater than or equal to 0.15 have been appended with the text "significant sector bias" to caution SMD users of a potential problem.

The experimenters with slides with significant sector bias were offered the opportunity to repeat these experiments.

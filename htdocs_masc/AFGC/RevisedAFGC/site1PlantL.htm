AFGC PLANT-SPECIFIC GENES

Updated August 1, 2002


Note 1: A Plant-Specific Genes website has been developed by the Michigan State University group (Pam Green, Ken Keegstra, John Ohlrogge) and will be maintained beyond the termination of the AFGC project.

         One objective of our project is to further our understanding of plant-specific genes, that is genes found in multiple plants but not other organisms. Our rationale for this emphasis is threefold. First, plant-specific genes have a high probability of functioning in plant-specific processes. Second, information about plant-specific genes should contribute to diverse areas of plant biology. Third, because plant-specific genes by definition exist in multiple plants, examining such genes in more detail will provide information and materials that should be useful for researchers working with many different plant species.

         This aspect of our project will focus on about 50 plant-specific genes chosen as described below. The expression of these genes will be examined under a variety of conditions using DNA microarrays. We will also screen for knockout mutations in these genes. Summaries of expression information and any visible phenotypes associated with the Knockout (KO) mutations for each plant-specific gene will be published on TAIR and/or this website for community access. Links to other databases that describe homologs of these genes in other plant species will be included.

         To identify plant-specific genes, the genes of Arabidopsis will be compared to genes of other plants and those of other organisms. A list of plant-specific genes, found in multiple plants but not other organisms, will be identified. Genes of known function will be removed from the list and the remaining genes of unknown function will be classified into the three categories below. The 50 plant-specific genes that will be the focus of AFGC studies will be selected from among the genes in these categories.


Categories of Plant-Specific Genes:

I. Plant-specific genes with no homology that suggests function.

II. Genes with suspected enzymatic, structural, or process association based on domain homologies or known location of gene product, but whose biological role is unknown.

III. Genes with homology to genes of known or suspected function that have in addition a plant-specific domain (i.e., a domain found in multiple plants, but not other organisms), of unknown function. Some genes may fulfill criteria of both categories I and II.

The plant-specific genes website will be maintained by the group at Michigan State University (John Ohlrogge, Ken Keegstra).


AFGC Experiments

         AFGC Personnel at Michigan State University and Carnegie Institution of Washington (CIW) will carry out several DNA microarray experiments to provide general information about plant-specific genes and other genes on AFGC arrays. These experiments will include expression studies of the major organs of Arabidopsis such as leaf, stem, root and flowers. In addition, at least twenty other array experiments will be performed to examine certain aspects of development (e.g., some stages of leaf, stem and flower development) and the response of Arabidopsis to selected stimuli (e.g., light, carbon dioxide, wounding, methyl jasmonate and circadian rhythms). All resulting data will be made publicly available through the Stanford Microarray Database.
